ANYmal X

Contributed to the development of ANYmal X, an autonomous quadruped robot designed and certified for operation in explosion-hazardous industrial environments.

Field

Robotics

Period

2022–2025

Focus

Mechanical engineering / Systems engineering

Overview

Developing an autonomous inspection robot for hazardous industrial environments.

ANYmal X was developed to perform autonomous inspection missions in environments where explosive gases may be present, reducing human exposure while collecting reliable operational data.

I contributed to the mechanical development, systems engineering and certification of the platform from early product development through industrialization.

As the project evolved, my role expanded from mechanical engineering into broader systems engineering responsibilities, including integration, verification, requirements engineering and certification.

Key challenges

Engineering a mobile robot for explosive environments.

The platform had to satisfy demanding mechanical, safety and certification requirements without compromising mobility, maintainability or autonomous operation.

  • Gas-tight enclosure design for Ex pxb protection
  • Explosion-proof Ex d drive & battery enclosure
  • Cable routing and gas-tight feedthrough connections for Ex pxb protection
  • Weight reduction while maintaining structural integrity
  • Thermal management of electronics and battery systems in a sealed enclosure
  • Impact protection for exposed components
  • Integration of safety and monitoring devices

Solution

A certified robotic platform combining mechanical protection, mobility and autonomous inspection.

The final platform combined gas-tight enclosures, explosion-protected drives, integrated sensing and safety systems within a mobile quadruped architecture.

My work included mechanical design, subsystem integration, design verification and coordination across interfaces between mechanics, electronics, software and certification requirements.

The development process required continuous iteration between design, testing and verification to ensure that the platform remained functional, serviceable and compliant throughout industrialization.

Reflection

What I took from the experience.

Building ANYmal X taught me that successful robotics is rarely about a single component. It emerges from the careful integration of mechanics, electronics, software and certification into one coherent system.

Working across these disciplines fundamentally shaped how I approach engineering today. It strengthened my ability to consider interfaces, requirements and verification alongside the design of individual components.

The project also reinforced the value of iteration: understanding a problem, testing an idea and improving it through observation. Moving from early development into industrialization showed me how engineering decisions must remain robust not only in prototypes, but also in production, service and real-world operation.
