Drone Development

Designed and realized a lightweight motor plate and deployable nose landing gear for the mobula 3.0 vertical take-off unmanned aerial vehicle, integrating propulsion, batteries, electronics and landing functionality into one compact structural assembly.

Field

Aerospace engineering

Period

2019

Focus

Product development / Mechanical design / Lightweight engineering

Overview

Developing a lightweight structural module for a vertical take-off UAV.

The mobula 3.0 was conceived as an unmanned aerial vehicle capable of vertical take-off and rapid deployment. The aircraft therefore required an extremely lightweight construction while still withstanding the mechanical loads generated during take-off, flight and landing.

My responsibility within the larger team project was the development of the motor plate and deployable nose landing gear. The assembly had to support the propulsion system, batteries, electronic speed controllers, camera equipment and electrical connections while integrating into the two longitudinal carbon-fibre structural members.

The project followed a complete product-development process, including requirements definition, concept generation, concept evaluation, CAD design, manufacturing, assembly and design optimization.

Key challenges

Combining low weight, structural stiffness and component integration.

The motor plate had to satisfy a large number of mechanical, electrical and aerodynamic requirements within a tightly constrained installation space.

  • Integration of the motor, batteries, camera, air unit and electronic speed controllers
  • Lightweight design under strict thrust and mass limitations
  • High bending and torsional stiffness during flight and landing
  • Development of a spring-deployed nose landing gear
  • Servo-controlled release of the landing-gear mechanism
  • Accessible battery replacement, connectors and service interfaces
  • Cable routing and packaging within a compact rib structure
  • Cooling of the camera, air unit and electrical components
  • Integration into the aerodynamic profile of the aircraft

Solution

An integrated ribbed motor plate with a deployable nose landing gear.

The final design combined the motor mounting structure, battery compartments, electronics packaging and landing-gear mechanism within a single modular assembly connected to the aircraft's carbon-fibre longitudinal members.

Lightweight construction was achieved through thin wall sections, internal cavities and a ribbed structural layout. The arrangement of the components was developed to maintain a balanced centre of gravity while keeping batteries, connectors and other service-relevant components accessible.

The nose landing gear used a preloaded spring mechanism that could be released by a servo motor. Its pivot and structural interfaces were integrated directly into the motor-plate assembly, allowing the gear to remain compact during flight and provide the required ground clearance after deployment.

The completed assembly demonstrated that the required components and landing gear could be integrated without introducing major structural weak points. The final assembly weighed approximately 2800 grams, exceeding the original 2400-gram target primarily because the initial mass allocation underestimated the contribution of the motor and batteries.

Flight testing could not be completed during the project due to delays and a separate failure during flight-controller testing. The remaining work therefore focused on assembly evaluation and recommendations concerning cable space, cooling, structural tolerances and landing-gear reliability.

Reflection

What I took from the experience.

This project was one of my first experiences developing a mechanically complex product from requirements through concept, detailed design, manufacturing and assembly. It showed me how strongly component packaging, structural design, accessibility and manufacturability influence one another.

The motor plate demonstrated the value of lightweight design methods such as rib structures, thin walls and integrated functionality. At the same time, the final mass showed that a lightweight component cannot be designed independently from an accurate system-level mass budget.

Assembly also revealed practical issues that were difficult to identify in CAD alone. Cable connectors, tolerances, cooling paths and access requirements required more space than initially expected and led to several modifications of the rib structure.

The inability to complete the planned flight tests reinforced the importance of project dependencies and staged validation. Even though the mechanical assembly could be designed and built, final system validation depended on the readiness and reliability of the complete aircraft.

Overall, the project strengthened my understanding of structured product development and taught me to consider weight, interfaces, assembly, testing and future optimization as part of one connected engineering process.
